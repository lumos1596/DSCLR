% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Instruction-following retrieval requires systems to adapt their ranking
when query instructions change. Existing query rewriting methods
(e.g., HyDE, Query2Doc, DeepRetrieval) improve raw retrieval quality
through semantic enhancement but lose instruction sensitivity
(p-MRR $\approx$ 0). We propose \method{}, which decomposes an
instruction into a positive enhancement query ($\qpos$) and a
negative exclusion query ($\qminus$), then applies a training-free
decision-layer scoring mechanism that estimates background leakage in
the negative channel and penalizes only residual exclusion evidence.
Our experiments show that
(1) inference-time score calibration derives query-specific
$\alpha_q$ and $\beta_q$ from candidate-set score distributions,
avoiding labeled validation data and test-set grid search; (2)
\method{} achieves the strongest average instruction sensitivity and
retrieval quality among the compared FollowIR methods; (3) additional
text-retrieval evaluations confirm that the decision-layer formulation
transfers beyond FollowIR; and (4) we
identify and analyze a \emph{semantic
entanglement} failure mode with a conservative prompting remedy.

\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Query rewriting has emerged as a powerful paradigm for improving dense
retrieval, with methods such as HyDE \cite{gao2023hyde}, Query2Doc
\cite{wang2023query2doc}, and DeepRetrieval \cite{li2025deepretrieval}
demonstrating substantial gains in retrieval quality by enriching the
query's semantic coverage.  Some methods even generate multiple query
variants --- RAG-Fusion \cite{raudaschl2024ragfusion} produces diverse
rephrasings and fuses their retrieval results.  However, all existing
rewriting methods share a critical limitation: their rewritten queries
are \textbf{semantically unidirectional} --- they all point toward
``what to retrieve'' but none creates an independent channel for
``what to exclude.''  Even RAG-Fusion's multiple variants are still
positive rephrasings of the same intent.  As a result, current
rewriting remains largely \textbf{insensitive to instruction changes}:
when the exclusion constraint in an instruction is modified, rankings
barely move and p-MRR stays near zero \cite{followir2024}.  Existing
rewriting methods can express \emph{where to look}, but they cannot
independently express \emph{where not to look}.

This limitation has a structural origin.  Real-world
retrieval instructions contain \textbf{three distinct types of
signals} --- background context, positive refinement, and negative
exclusion \cite{followir2024} --- yet existing rewriting pipelines only
strengthen the first two.  They can describe relevance more richly, but
they still collapse all instruction signals into a single retrieval
direction.  The challenge is therefore not only to generate a negative
query, but to distinguish exclusion evidence that warrants suppression
from negative-channel similarity induced by topical background overlap.

Prior work attacks this problem from two other directions, but both have
clear limitations.  Supervised instruction-following retrievers such as
Promptriever \cite{weller2024promptriever}, InF-IR
\cite{zhuang2025infir}, and Dual-View Training \cite{dualview2026}
improve instruction following through fine-tuning on annotated or
synthesized pairs, but the data cost is substantial and generalization
to unseen exclusion types remains uncertain.  Representation-layer
negation methods such as DEO \cite{deo2024,deo2026} explicitly model
positive and negative components, but resolve them through vector-space
operations such as $Q_{\text{base}} - Q_{\text{neg}}$, which couples
suppression with distortion of the positive intent.  In both cases,
instruction resolution still happens primarily at the
\textbf{representation layer}.

We instead argue that instruction-following retrieval needs a
\textbf{decision-layer} treatment.  The failure of positive-only
rewriting is not that the rewrites are weak, but that they are
structurally incomplete: no collection of positive rephrasings can mark
out a semantic boundary around documents targeted for exclusion.
Moreover, relevant documents and ``trap documents'' often share topic,
entities, and vocabulary, so raw negative similarity is itself
confounded by background overlap.  The solution is therefore twofold:
create an explicit exclusion channel, and interpret that channel
through a query-adaptive boundary that discounts background leakage
before applying suppression.

We propose \method{} (\textbf{De}composition-based
\textbf{I}nstruction-\textbf{R}ewriting with \textbf{Dual}-track
scoring), a training-free framework built around these two ideas.
First, \method{} decomposes each instruction into a base query
($\qbase$), a positive refinement query ($\qpos$), and a negative
exclusion query ($\qminus$), then applies a \textbf{query-adaptive
exclusion boundary}: it estimates how much of the negative-channel score
is expected from background relevance and penalizes only the residual
excess beyond that estimate.  The same residual also gates the positive
reward, so documents are helped by the refinement channel only when
their negative evidence remains within the safe region.  Second,
\method{} uses \textbf{label-free inference-time score calibration} to
derive query-specific reward and penalty scales directly from the
candidate-set score distributions, avoiding labeled validation data and
benchmark-specific grid search.

Rather than simply producing stronger query rewrites, \method{} converts
positive-only rewriting into exclusion-aware reranking without altering
the retriever.  On FollowIR
\cite{followir2024}, \method{} improves both instruction sensitivity and
retrieval quality over query rewriting and retriever baselines.  On
additional negation-sensitive and general-domain benchmarks, the same
decision-layer formulation transfers without per-dataset supervision.
Finally, diagnostic analysis identifies \textbf{semantic entanglement}
--- heavy overlap between the base and exclusion channels --- as the key
failure mode that limits training-free exclusion reasoning.

In summary, we make two technical contributions and one empirical
observation: \textbf{(i)} a query-adaptive exclusion boundary that
converts triple-query decomposition into selective decision-layer
suppression; \textbf{(ii)} a label-free inference-time calibration
scheme that aligns the boundary and reward scales to each query's
candidate-set statistics; and \textbf{(iii)} an empirical
characterization of semantic entanglement as the main boundary-failure
regime.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\subsection{LLM-Based Query Rewriting and Semantic Enhancement}

LLM-based query rewriting improves retrieval by making the user's
information need more explicit before matching.  HyDE
\cite{gao2023hyde} retrieves with hypothetical documents, Query2Doc
\cite{wang2023query2doc} enriches queries with pseudo-documents, and
RAG-Fusion \cite{raudaschl2024ragfusion} combines multiple generated
query variants.  More recent rewriting systems learn the rewriting
policy or optimize retrieval-oriented generation, including RAG-QR
\cite{ma2023ragqr} and DeepRetrieval
\cite{li2025deepretrieval}.  These methods primarily improve recall or
semantic coverage, but they do not explicitly model exclusion as a
separate decision signal.  Even when multiple rewrites are generated,
the variants usually remain positive descriptions of relevance rather
than independent evidence for what should be suppressed.  Our method
may use query decomposition to obtain positive and negative channels,
but moves beyond rewriting by introducing an explicit exclusion-aware
decision layer.

\subsection{Instruction-Following Retrieval with Exclusion Constraints}

Instruction-following retrieval extends dense retrieval from topical
matching to instruction-conditioned matching.  Instruction-tuned
retrievers such as INSTRUCTOR \cite{instructor2022} and TART
\cite{tart2022} train embedding models with task descriptions, while
larger instruction-aware retrievers such as E5-Mistral
\cite{e5mistral2023}, BGE/C-Pack \cite{bge2023}, GritLM
\cite{gritlm2024}, Promptriever \cite{weller2024promptriever}, and
InF-IR \cite{zhuang2025infir} improve instruction sensitivity through
instruction-labeled or synthetic supervision.  FollowIR
\cite{followir2024} further formalizes per-query instruction following
through original/changed query pairs and metrics such as p-MRR, while
Dual-View Training \cite{dualview2026} synthesizes polarity-reversal
data to improve sensitivity to instruction changes.  These works show
that instruction-following retrieval can be improved through training,
but training-centric approaches require retriever updates and
additional instruction data, and they must balance instruction
sensitivity against broad retrieval generalization.

Exclusion constraints are a particularly challenging form of
instruction following.  In exclusion queries, the hardest negatives are
not irrelevant documents but topically relevant documents that violate
the constraint.  Such documents may match the affirmative intent and
share substantial background with the query, yet violate the user's
``not'', ``without'', or ``exclude'' condition.  This makes positive
query enhancement alone less suited to the problem: it can strengthen
topical relevance without identifying which relevant-looking documents
should be suppressed.

One line of work addresses negative constraints and complex logical
queries by making constraint structure explicit.  Neural-symbolic
retrieval methods such as NS-IR \cite{xu2025logical} convert
natural-language queries and documents into first-order logic forms and
enforce logical consistency through reranking objectives such as logic
alignment and connective constraints.  OrLog \cite{hoveyda2026orlog}
instead decomposes complex queries into atomic predicates, estimates
predicate-level plausibility with LLMs, and applies probabilistic
reasoning to infer query satisfaction.  These routes offer
interpretable ways to represent logical constraints, but they introduce
a different dependency: natural-language retrieval must be converted
into logical or predicate-level representations, or coupled with
symbolic/probabilistic reasoning objectives.  Our method stays in the
dense score space and does not require a logical parser, symbolic
inference module, or probabilistic reasoning engine.

A closer line of work avoids retriever training by decomposing
instructions into positive and negative components and modifying query
representations at inference time.  DEO-style methods
\cite{deo2024,deov2,deo2026} learn or construct positive and negative
instruction components, with training-free variants operating at the
representation layer through query embedding modification or
optimization.  Representation-layer methods change the query vector
globally, which can make it difficult to distinguish true exclusion
evidence from background overlap at the document level.  In contrast,
\method{} keeps the retriever fixed, avoids symbolic query conversion,
and resolves exclusion at the decision layer.  By estimating background
leakage and calibrating reward/penalty scales from the current
unlabeled candidate set, it targets constraint satisfaction without
retriever fine-tuning or global query-vector manipulation.

\subsection{Query-Local Score Calibration and Test-Time Adaptation}

The calibration component of \method{} is related to post-hoc
calibration and local similarity correction.  Platt scaling and
temperature scaling transform model outputs after training while
keeping model parameters fixed
\cite{platt1999probabilistic,guo2017temperature}, but are typically
estimated from labeled validation data for confidence calibration.
Local scaling methods such as CSLS \cite{conneau2018wordtranslation}
show a complementary principle: raw similarities can contain local
bias, and neighborhood statistics can improve comparability across
candidates.  Our setting differs from both lines because it requires
label-free calibration of ranking scores for each query.

Information retrieval also provides a direct precedent for using
unlabeled query-local evidence.  Query performance prediction estimates
the expected reliability of a ranking without relevance judgments, and
recent studies show that neural rankers exhibit query-specific
behaviors that are difficult to capture with traditional predictors
\cite{faggioli2023query}.  Pseudo-relevance feedback similarly uses the
top-ranked set as local evidence, including in dense retrieval settings
where feedback representations are extracted from first-pass results
\cite{wang2021pseudo}.  \method{} adopts the same broad test-time
principle but uses the candidate set differently: it does not predict
query difficulty or expand the query, but estimates the score scales of
the base, positive, and negative channels.

Recent inference-only calibration work further shows that unlabeled
test batches can be used to estimate and correct score biases without
supervised validation labels \cite{zhou2024batch}.  In contrast,
\method{} does not tune prompts, update model parameters, or learn
global calibration constants.  It keeps the retriever fixed and uses
candidate-set statistics only to calibrate decision-layer reward and
penalty scales.

\section{Method: DeIR-Dual}
\label{sec:method}

% Section narrative: We first formalize why instruction-following
% retrieval needs independent channels, then introduce triple-query
% decomposition, present the query-adaptive exclusion boundary, and
% finally describe the label-free inference-time calibration that
% sets the reward and penalty scales.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem-formulation}

Dense retrievers typically encode a query--instruction pair into a
single vector and rank documents by cosine similarity.  In
instruction-following retrieval, however, the instruction may contain
signals with different roles:
%
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Background context}: information that frames the user's
    information need and helps interpret the instruction.
  \item \textbf{Positive refinement}: affirmative constraints that specify
    what should be emphasized or made more specific.
  \item \textbf{Negative exclusion}: constraints that specify what should be
    suppressed or treated as irrelevant.
\end{enumerate}
%
When these signals are collapsed into one embedding, the resulting score
provides a single relevance direction.  This score can be effective for
topical matching, but it does not expose separate controls for
contextual relevance, positive refinement, and negative exclusion.  The
issue is reflected in changed-instruction evaluations such as FollowIR
\cite{followir2024}, where a system must respond to fine-grained
query-level constraints rather than only retrieve topically related
documents.

We formulate the problem as inference-time decision-layer reranking with
a frozen retriever.  Given a query and instruction, the goal is to
construct separate scoring channels for contextual relevance, positive
refinement, and negative exclusion without updating the encoder.  The
negative channel is then converted into a query-adaptive exclusion
boundary rather than used as a raw penalty.

\subsection{Triple-Query Decomposition}
\label{sec:dual-query-generation}

\method{} constructs three query channels that provide the decision
layer with separate evidence for context, affirmative refinement, and
exclusion:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{$\qbase$ (Base Query)} contains the original query and the
    full instruction.  It serves as the contextual relevance anchor that
    preserves the broader information need.
  \item \textbf{$\qpos$ (Positive Refinement)} extracts the affirmative
    constraints that refine what should be retrieved.  This channel
    emphasizes positive requirements, while the base channel retains the
    broader context.
  \item \textbf{$\qminus$ (Negative Exclusion)} extracts the excluded
    content and defines the negative channel used for boundary
    estimation.
\end{enumerate}

The three queries are independently encoded by the same dense retriever
$f(\cdot)$, producing three query embeddings
$\mathbf{h}_{\text{base}} = f(\qbase)$,
$\mathbf{h}_{\text{pos}} = f(\qpos)$,
$\mathbf{h}_{\text{neg}} = f(\qminus)$.
Document embeddings $\mathbf{v}_d = f(d)$ are pre-computed once and reused.

The base channel is not used to enforce exclusion directly; it provides
the contextual relevance anchor against which the positive and negative
channels are calibrated.  This role is important because a
positive/negative decomposition alone may remove contextual information
that determines how refinement and exclusion should be interpreted.  We
therefore keep the full instruction as a context anchor and use the
positive and negative queries as targeted decision-layer signals.

In practice, the decomposer is prompted to produce short
retrieval-style queries and to place affirmative and excluded
constraints in separate fields.  Prompts and examples are provided in
the appendix.

\subsection{Query-Adaptive Exclusion Boundary}
\label{sec:scoring-formula}

Given the three query encodings, we compute document-level cosine
similarities
%
\begin{equation}
  \sbase(d) = \cos(\mathbf{h}_{\text{base}}, \mathbf{v}_d),\quad
  \spos(d) = \cos(\mathbf{h}_{\text{pos}}, \mathbf{v}_d),\quad
  \sneg(d) = \cos(\mathbf{h}_{\text{neg}}, \mathbf{v}_d).
  \label{eq:channel-scores}
\end{equation}
%
The base and positive channels provide topical relevance and
instructional refinement.  The negative channel is more delicate: a
high $\sneg(d)$ can indicate a true constraint violation, but it can
also arise because relevant and excluded documents share entities,
topic, or background context.  \method{} therefore treats exclusion as
a \textbf{residual boundary estimation} problem.  Negative-channel
similarity is decomposed into background leakage and residual exclusion
evidence.  We estimate the background component from the current
query's candidate distribution, subtract it from the negative-channel
score, and penalize residual evidence only as it approaches and exceeds
a robust query-specific boundary.

\subsubsection{Background Leakage Estimation}

Our treatment follows the same broad intuition as local scaling methods
for nearest-neighbor retrieval: raw similarity can contain a query-local
bias that should be removed before comparison
\cite{conneau2018wordtranslation}.  In our setting, this bias is
background-induced similarity in the negative channel.  We use a simple
query-local linear leakage model to estimate the part of the
negative-channel score that is expected from background relevance.  Let
$\mu_b,\sigma_b$ be the mean and standard deviation of
$\sbase$ over the candidate set, and let $\mu_n,\sigma_n$ be the
corresponding statistics for $\sneg$.  We first standardize the base
score,
%
\begin{equation}
  z_{\text{base}}(d) =
    \frac{\sbase(d) - \mu_b}{\sigma_b},
  \label{eq:base-zscore}
\end{equation}
%
and measure the query-level overlap between the base and exclusion
channels as
%
\begin{equation}
  c_q = \cos(\mathbf{h}_{\text{base}}, \mathbf{h}_{\text{neg}}).
  \label{eq:query-overlap}
\end{equation}
%
The expected negative-channel score induced by background relevance is
then
%
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{S}^{\text{bg}}_{\text{neg}}(d)
    = \mu_n + \sigma_n\, c_q\, z_{\text{base}}(d).
  \label{eq:bg-estimate}
\end{equation}
%
This estimate transfers the standardized base-channel variation into
the negative-channel scale, modulated by the semantic overlap between
the base and exclusion queries.  It is not intended as a generative
model of relevance; it is a query-local baseline for the negative score
expected from background overlap.

\subsubsection{Residual Exclusion Boundary}

We define the residual negative score as the excess negative-channel
evidence beyond the background estimate:
%
\begin{equation}
  R_{\text{neg}}(d)
    = \sneg(d) - \widehat{S}^{\text{bg}}_{\text{neg}}(d).
  \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
%
Positive residuals indicate that the document matches the exclusion
query more strongly than expected from background relevance alone.  We
define the query-adaptive exclusion boundary with the median and median
absolute deviation of the residual scores:
%
\begin{equation}
  \tau_q =
  \operatorname{median}(\mathcal{R}_q)
  + \lambda \operatorname{MAD}(\mathcal{R}_q),\quad
  \mathcal{R}_q=\{R_{\text{neg}}(d):d\in\mathcal{C}_q\},
  \label{eq:mad-margin}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{C}_q$ denotes the retrieved candidate set.  Residuals
below $\tau_q$ are treated as background-consistent, while residuals
above $\tau_q$ provide stronger evidence for exclusion.  We use
Softplus as a smooth approximation to a hinge penalty:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{penalty}(d)
    = \alpha_q\,\operatorname{Softplus}\!\left(R_{\text{neg}}(d)-\tau_q\right).
  \label{eq:residual-penalty}
\end{equation}
%
The penalty grows smoothly as residual exclusion evidence approaches
and exceeds the boundary, while the residual construction prevents the
negative channel from penalizing documents merely because they share
topical background with the exclusion query.  The scale $\alpha_q$ is
derived by score calibration in \S\ref{sec:parameter-derivation}.

\subsubsection{Safety-Gated Positive Reward}

The positive channel can improve recall for documents satisfying the
instruction, but a raw positive reward can also amplify documents that
violate the exclusion constraint.  We therefore gate the positive
reward with respect to the same residual boundary:
%
\begin{equation}
  g_q(d)
    = 1 - \sigma\!\left(
      \kappa \frac{R_{\text{neg}}(d)-\tau_q}
      {\operatorname{MAD}(\mathcal{R}_q)}
    \right).
  \label{eq:safety}
\end{equation}
%
The gate largely preserves the positive reward for documents whose residuals fall below the boundary and progressively suppresses it as residual exclusion evidence crosses the boundary.  The fixed shape parameter $\kappa$ controls the
sharpness of this transition.

\textbf{Final score.}
The final decision-layer score combines base relevance, safety-gated
positive refinement, and residual exclusion penalty:
%
  \begin{equation}
  \sfinal(d)
    = \sbase(d)
    + \beta_q\,\spos(d)\,g_q(d)
    - \alpha_q\,\operatorname{Softplus}\!\left(R_{\text{neg}}(d)-\tau_q\right).
  \label{eq:final}
\end{equation}
%
This nonlinear score composition implements a query-adaptive exclusion
boundary at ranking time without modifying the dense retriever.  The
parameters $\alpha_q$ and $\beta_q$ are derived from the score
distributions of the current query, while $\lambda$ and $\kappa$
are fixed shape parameters controlling boundary offset and gate
sharpness.

% 3.4 INFERENCE-TIME SCORE CALIBRATION
% ============================================================
\subsection{Inference-Time Score Calibration}
\label{sec:parameter-derivation}

The scoring formula in Eq.~\ref{eq:final} contains two query-level
calibration factors: $\alpha_q$ for the residual exclusion penalty and
$\beta_q$ for the positive refinement reward.  Rather than
grid-searching these values on labeled queries, we derive them at
reranking time from the score distributions of the current candidate
set.  This converts parameter selection into query-conditioned score
calibration: the correction terms are placed on a magnitude comparable
to the base retriever without changing the retriever or using relevance
labels.  The goal is not to fit a benchmark-specific optimum, but to
make the exclusion boundary and positive reward portable across
retrievers with different score scales.

\subsubsection{Candidate-Set Score Calibration}
\label{sec:scale-alignment}

The base, positive, and negative channels are produced by the same dense
retriever but have different score distributions because they encode
different query intents.  We therefore treat $\alpha_q$ and $\beta_q$
as post-hoc scale factors for decision-layer reranking.  Unlike
validation-set calibration methods such as Platt scaling and
temperature scaling
\cite{platt1999probabilistic,guo2017temperature}, our calibration is
label-free, query-local, and operates on ranking scores.  The only
inputs are the channel scores over the current candidate set
$\mathcal{C}_q$.

Let the residual excess over the boundary be
%
\begin{equation}
  o_q(d) = R_{\text{neg}}(d) - \tau_q,
  \label{eq:residual-excess}
\end{equation}
%
and define the residual-exceeding subset as
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{A}_q =
  \{d \in \mathcal{C}_q : o_q(d) > 0\}.
  \label{eq:exceeding-set}
\end{equation}
%
These are the documents whose negative residual exceeds the robust
boundary and therefore carries residual exclusion evidence.  We write
the corresponding penalty magnitude as
%
\begin{equation}
  P_q(d) = \operatorname{Softplus}(o_q(d)).
  \label{eq:penalty-magnitude}
\end{equation}
%
Let $E_b(d)$ and $E_{\text{pos}}(d)$ denote nonnegative evidence
magnitudes for the base and positive-refinement channels.  These
magnitudes are used only for scale estimation.  We derive $\alpha_q$
and $\beta_q$ by active-region scale matching: each correction term is
scaled so that its average magnitude on the region where it is active
is comparable to the average base evidence on that same region.
For the penalty-active region, this gives
%
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_q =
    \frac{
      \operatorname*{Avg}_{d\in\mathcal{A}_q} E_b(d)
    }{
      \operatorname*{Avg}_{d\in\mathcal{A}_q} P_q(d)
    }.
  \label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}
%
For the positive channel, we use the complementary safe subset
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{B}_q =
  \{d \in \mathcal{C}_q : o_q(d) \leq 0\}.
  \label{eq:safe-set}
\end{equation}
%
The reward coefficient is derived after applying the safety gate, since
the positive reward is reduced when a document has residual negative
evidence.  We define the gated positive-refinement evidence as
%
\begin{equation}
  E_q^+(d) = g_q(d)E_{\text{pos}}(d).
  \label{eq:gated-positive-evidence}
\end{equation}
%
The same scale-matching principle calibrates the reward-active region:
the average gated positive-refinement evidence in $\mathcal{B}_q$ is
matched to the average base evidence on $\mathcal{B}_q$:
%
\begin{equation}
  \beta_q =
    \frac{
      \operatorname*{Avg}_{d\in\mathcal{B}_q} E_b(d)
    }{
      \operatorname*{Avg}_{d\in\mathcal{B}_q} E_q^+(d)
    }.
  \label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
%
Degenerate cases are handled by fixed label-free defaults.

\subsubsection{Fixed Shape Parameters}
\label{sec:residual-calibration}

The main scale parameters, $\alpha_q$ and $\beta_q$, are derived from
the current query's score statistics.  By contrast, $\lambda$ and
$\kappa$ are fixed shape constants: $\lambda$ controls how far the
boundary is placed above the residual median, and $\kappa$ controls the
sharpness of the gate around that boundary.  They are fixed across
queries and retrievers, and sensitivity analysis examines their effect.

\subsubsection{Label-Free Inference-Time Derivation}
\label{sec:train-vs-test}

A central risk in inference-time calibration is test-set leakage:
parameters must not be selected using relevance labels, evaluation
metrics, or benchmark-level grid search.  Our derivation avoids this
leakage because $\alpha_q$ and $\beta_q$ are computed only from the
unlabeled channel scores in $\mathcal{C}_q$.  The retrieval labels are
never used when computing Eqs.~\ref{eq:alpha}--\ref{eq:beta}.
%
The remaining constants $\lambda$ and $\kappa$ define the boundary
offset and gate sharpness.  They are fixed across queries and analyzed
separately in ablation and sensitivity studies.  This separation keeps
the method portable across dense retrievers: query-level calibration
adapts to the score scale of the current retriever, while global shape
constants are not re-selected for individual test queries.

\section{Conclusion and Limitations}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented \method{}, a frozen-retriever decision-layer
reranking method for text retrieval under exclusion-sensitive
instructions.  The central idea is to avoid treating negative-query
similarity as uniformly harmful.  \method{} first uses triple-query
decomposition to separate contextual relevance, positive refinement, and
exclusion evidence; it then estimates a query-adaptive residual
exclusion boundary over the candidate set and applies label-free
query-local score calibration to place residual penalties and gated
positive rewards on comparable scales.  The current FollowIR results
show improvements in instruction sensitivity and aggregate retrieval
quality over inference-time rewriting and reranking baselines, while the
cross-retriever, transfer, ablation, and appendix sensitivity analyses
are designed to test whether these gains persist across frozen
retrievers, datasets, mechanism variants, and fixed shape constants once
their remaining entries are completed.

The scope of \method{} is bounded by several limitations.  It depends
on LLM-generated decompositions, so ambiguous or underspecified
instructions can propagate errors into the positive and negative
channels.  High-overlap cases, where desired and excluded semantics are
tightly entangled, remain difficult because residual exclusion evidence
can be hard to distinguish from background relevance.  The fixed shape
constants $\lambda$ and $\kappa$ are intended to improve portability,
but their stability still requires the planned sensitivity validation.
Finally, because \method{} reranks an existing candidate set rather than
retrieving new documents, its effectiveness depends on the base
retriever placing relevant documents in $\mathcal{C}_q$; missed
candidates cannot be recovered by the decision-layer scorer.
